VPNs for a layperson

A cybersecurity researcher's attempt to simplify VPNs and their usecases

I intend for this to be part of a series of posts, each outlining my stance and my opinion on various technologies and whether popular opinion online seems to contradict or support my stance. I would, of course, separate my opinion from fact, and in the end, it’s up to the reader to take up my advice or even believe me.

This was created to collate my thoughts on how to maintain a decent level of privacy online.

Introduction

As is required when talking about something as abstract as “internet privacy”, the first question that should arrive in your mind is: “Privacy from whom?”. Questions like these are together called “Threat modelling”, and there are some great articles on this by much better authorities, so I will defer to them. But I will, however, copy the same questions and assume some answers.

The Six Questions

The EFF has a simple list of six questions to answer:

  • What do I want to protect?

A: Since you already give away some personal data to various applications and websites while browsing the internet, the assumption I make here is that you intend to protect either the aforementioned data (let’s call this Scenario 1) or you would want to protect other data that you do not explicitly give permission for anyone to view. This might be data that is for private viewing only (Scenario 2). A good example of the second kind of data would be fingerprinting information, which might be used to gauge your financial habits.

  • Who do I want to protect it from?

A: For each of these scenarios, there are different types of entities or people that you might want to protect it from. In Scenario 1, you wouldn’t want this data to get into the hands of any criminal or third party that isn’t supposed to get them. Common examples given by certain companies include your ISP or people intending to steal your data for some or the other purpose.

Scenario 2 is a bit more broad, and it would probably include the very companies whom you willingly give some of your data to. As shown in the substack link linked above, sometimes the very apps that you install on your phone want to know more than you’re willing to let them know. This is the reality of the internet that we live in, and we would need to work because of it.
